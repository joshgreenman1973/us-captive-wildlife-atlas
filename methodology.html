Methodology

What's on the map, where the species data comes from, and what's deliberately missing.

Scope

This atlas shows every AZA-accredited zoo and aquarium in the United States — 215 institutions — with searchable species inventories scraped from each zoo's own public website.

An earlier version included 2,500+ USDA-licensed exhibitors and dealers. That dataset was dropped. The USDA list is heavily polluted with regulated pet operations (kennels, catteries, puppy mills, pet shops) that USDA's Animal Welfare Act covers alongside wildlife exhibitors. More importantly, those facilities don't publish their inventories, so species data for them isn't available without parsing inspection PDFs at scale. AZA accreditation is an imperfect but defensible quality floor: institutions that have passed a peer-reviewed accreditation process and that maintain public-facing animal lists.

Where the species data comes from

For each AZA institution, the project fetches the zoo's public "Our Animals" / "A–Z animals" / "Animals & Exhibits" page and extracts the species names listed there. Common URL patterns: /animals, /our-animals, /animals-a-z, /animals/list, /explore/animals, /discover/animals.

Coverage grows as more zoo sites are scraped. Some zoos block automated fetches; some don't publish a public A–Z list at all (those zoos still appear on the map but with no species details).

Species data reflects what each zoo chooses to publish on its website, which may lag actual current holdings (births, deaths, transfers). Common names are kept as the zoo writes them — they are not normalized to scientific binomials. Searching for "tiger" matches "Malayan Tiger", "Amur Tiger", and "Tiger" as separate species entries.

Geocoding precision

Markers show the centroid of each facility's city. AZA does not publish street addresses for every member, and city precision is fine for a national-scale atlas. Facilities that share a city (e.g., the four Brooklyn institutions) are spread on a small ring around the centroid so they don't pile up.

What's NOT on the map (and why)

Non-AZA zoos and roadside facilities

There are ~2,000 USDA Class C exhibitors that aren't AZA-accredited — drive-through safaris, roadside zoos, traveling exhibitors, small educational collections. Most don't publish species inventories, so they're not on this map. AZA accreditation is in no way a guarantee that every animal in a zoo is well cared for, but it's the cleanest available filter.

ZIMS (Species360)

The world's most comprehensive captive-animal database is ZIMS, run by Species360. We do not use ZIMS. Species360's terms explicitly prohibit publishing institution-level species holdings.

Privately held exotic animals and sanctuaries

The 2022 Big Cat Public Safety Act created a USFWS registry of privately held big cats — that layer is planned for a later phase. Other privately held exotics aren't on any federal registry. GFAS-accredited sanctuaries are also not yet included.

Animals beyond what each zoo publishes

A zoo's "Our Animals" page typically highlights its featured species and may omit small reptiles, fish, or invertebrates that live in its ecosystems. Absence from the map's species list does NOT mean the zoo doesn't hold the animal — only that we couldn't find it on the public page.

Reproducing the dataset

Build scripts in scripts/:

  • build_dataset.py — geocodes the AZA institution list against the Census places gazetteer and writes data/facilities.json.
  • build_species_index.py — reads data/zoo-species-raw.json, classifies each species into a vertebrate class and a "browse" group, attaches species lists to facilities, and writes data/species_index.json.

Caveats, in plain English

  • This is a browseable index, not a census. Use it to discover where a species can be seen, then verify with the zoo directly.
  • If a search returns no results, the species may exist at zoos we haven't scraped yet — coverage is rolling.
  • The species-name search is fuzzy (substring), so "tiger" finds tiger, Malayan Tiger, Amur Tiger, etc. The "Mammals" / "Big cats" chips are coarser and are computed from common-name keywords.
