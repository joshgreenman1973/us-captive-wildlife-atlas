Methodology & sources

How this atlas was made

Every number, every species list, every recording — where it came from, what's missing, and how to reproduce it.

Where each species list comes from

Every facility popup tells you whether its species list came from the zoo's own published list, Wikipedia, or both. We try the zoo's site first; we fall back to Wikipedia only when the zoo doesn't publish a public A–Z page (about a third of AZA institutions don't).

Source 1 — Each zoo's own animal pages

Common URL patterns scraped: /animals, /our-animals, /animals-a-z, /explore/animals, /visit/animals, /discover/animals. Species names are kept exactly as the zoo writes them — common names are not normalized to scientific binomials, which is why "Tiger", "Amur Tiger", "Malayan Tiger", and "Sumatran Tiger" each appear as separate entries (the zoos write them that way).

This is the highest-trust source: it's the institution itself describing its collection. It's also a snapshot of what the zoo chose to feature on its public-facing pages, so it may lag actual current holdings (births, deaths, transfers since the page was last updated).

Source 2 — Wikipedia (for zoos with no public A–Z list)

About a third of AZA institutions — including most of the WCS-run NYC zoos (Bronx, Central Park, Prospect Park, Queens, NY Aquarium), the Smithsonian, Disney's Animal Kingdom, Saint Louis, Memphis, Fort Worth, Dallas Zoo, and others — publish only featured-animal teasers on their websites, not full inventories. For these we fall back to the species lists on each zoo's Wikipedia article.

Wikipedia is collaboratively edited, so its species lists are second-hand: typically compiled from press releases, signage photos, and visitor reports. Less authoritative than the zoo's own page, but better than nothing. Each Wikipedia-sourced popup is labeled as such.

What the species lists are NOT: they are not census counts. They tell you which species have appeared on the institution's public-facing materials. They do not say how many individuals of each species are present, what proportion of the collection is on display, or whether a particular animal is still alive on the day you visit.

Why some major zoos still aren't well-covered

A few institutions fight the scraper hard: their pages are JavaScript-rendered (no static HTML), they 403 automated requests, or they simply don't keep a public list. Where neither the zoo nor Wikipedia offered a usable list, the facility appears on the map as a regular marker but its popup says "No public A–Z animal list found at this institution." That isn't a failure of the zoo — many AZA institutions have outstanding collections and active conservation programs without publishing an A–Z page. It's a transparency gap, not an absence.

Coordinates

All 215 markers are at street-level precise coordinates, hand-curated in data/zoo-coordinates.json from each zoo's published address (Wikipedia infoboxes, Apple Maps, the zoo's contact pages). An audit pass found nine errors larger than 2 km — biggest was Lee G. Simmons Wildlife Safari Park, off by 19 km because its Ashland, NE location is at the I-80 exit, not the city of Wahoo where it had been placed.

Named individuals

The Names tab shows specific animals (gorillas, elephants, orcas, etc.) scraped from each zoo's "Meet our [species]" or "Animal ambassadors" pages. SeaWorld and other cetacean parks are well-documented in Ceta-Base, an independent public database of captive cetaceans, which we used as a supplementary source. ~60% of records have published DOBs; ~40% don't.

Birds, reptiles, and aquarium residents are barely represented. Zoos publish bios primarily for charismatic megafauna where individual identity is part of the visitor draw. That's a real gap in any captive-animal database — most named individuals at AZA institutions don't have public bios at all.

News & events

The News tab is a 24-month rolling window of birth, death, hatching, arrival, and departure announcements from major zoos' press / blog pages. Where official press feeds were inaccessible, we supplemented from local TV / wire stories citing the zoo's own announcements (these are flagged in the underlying data). Aquarium events are underrepresented — Georgia Aquarium, Shedd, Monterey Bay, National Aquarium, and New England Aquarium either block scraping or don't publish dated birth/death announcements as discrete press items.

Animal sounds

Speaker icons next to species names play short audio recordings. All sounds are licensed for reuse — public domain, CC0, CC-BY, or CC-BY-SA — sourced from Wikimedia Commons with a few from xeno-canto for bird recordings. Each playback shows the credit and links back to the source page.

A substring matcher means searching "Amur Tiger" or "Sumatran Tiger" plays the generic Tiger recording when no species-specific clip is available. Coverage is currently 48 species and growing.

What's deliberately NOT in the atlas

Non-AZA zoos and roadside facilities

An earlier version of this project included 2,500+ USDA-licensed exhibitors and dealers — most regulated under the Animal Welfare Act. That dataset is heavily polluted with regulated pet operations (kennels, catteries, pet shops, puppy mills) that USDA's AWA covers alongside wildlife exhibitors. Most non-AZA exhibitors don't publish their inventories, so species data isn't available without parsing inspection PDFs. The dataset was dropped to keep the project focused.

ZIMS (Species360)

The world's most comprehensive captive-animal database is ZIMS, which holds individual records (DOBs, sex, parentage, transfers) for every animal at over 1,300 member institutions. We do not use ZIMS. Species360's terms explicitly prohibit publishing institution-level species holdings.

Privately held exotic animals

Most privately owned exotic mammals, reptiles, and birds are on no federal registry. The 2022 Big Cat Public Safety Act created a USFWS registry of privately held big cats; that layer is planned for a later phase but not currently shown.

Reproducing the dataset

Build scripts (Python 3, no extra deps beyond openpyxl):

  • scripts/build_dataset.py — geocodes the AZA institution list using data/zoo-coordinates.json overrides + the 2023 Census places gazetteer fallback. Writes data/facilities.json.
  • scripts/build_species_index.py — merges data/zoo-species-raw.json (zoo-website scrapes) and data/wikipedia-supplemental.json (Wikipedia backfill), tags each species with its source, classifies each into a vertebrate class and a "browse" group, attaches species lists to facilities, and writes data/species_index.json.

All raw scrape outputs are committed to the repository so anyone can audit which URLs were scraped and which species were extracted from each.

Caveats, plain English

  • This is a browseable index, not a census. Use it to discover where a species can be seen, then verify with the institution directly.
  • If a search returns no results, the species may exist at zoos we haven't covered yet. Coverage is rolling.
  • The species-name search is fuzzy (substring), so "tiger" finds Tiger, Malayan Tiger, Amur Tiger, Sumatran Tiger, Tiger Salamander, and Sand Tiger Shark as separate entries.
  • Common names are not normalized. Each zoo's spelling is preserved.
  • "AZA-accredited" means a zoo passed AZA's peer-reviewed accreditation process. It is not a guarantee that every animal in a zoo is well cared for, nor that every well-run American zoo is on this map (some excellent institutions are not AZA members by choice).
